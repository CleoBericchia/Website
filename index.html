Factors contributing to the widening health inequalities in the UK

This story examines the key factors involved in health inequalities in the UK and how they contribute to a widening gap across the population. It examines how the cost of living crisis has negatively affected exercise, particularly through rising costs and the impact of deprivation on maintaining a healthy lifestyle.

Health inequalities in the UK

Health inequalities are the unavoidable and unfair disparities in health across the population and groups within society. Health inequalities are shaped by social determinants such as income, education and housing.

In the UK, approximately 21% of the population lives in poverty and research shows that these individuals are likely to experience poor health, limited access to healthcare, and worse overall health outcomes. There are evident health inequalities between those living in poverty and those who are not and these inequalities stem from several factors, particularly socio-economic status and deprivation, including unemployment, low income and living in deprived areas.

Across the UK, health inequalities remain pronounced, particularly in the North of England, where people face a 20% greater risk of premature death, with economic inactivity, poverty and substandard housing playing key roles. Health inequalities in Scotland are closely linked to socioeconomic disparities, including income and related factors such as housing and living standards. Life expectancy in Belfast is the lowest in Northern Ireland and is considerably lower than in more affluent UK regions.

Health inequalities and physical activity

Health inequalities are evident in physical activity patterns, with the UK government identifying clear differences between those living in the most and least deprived areas. From 2015-2016 and 2023-2024, physical inactivity among adults increased in the most deprived areas of England, while it declines in the least deprived areas. By 2023-2024, adults in the most deprived areas were 2.3 times more likely to be physically inactive than those in the least deprived areas.

Level of exercise in the UK

Physical activity offers many benefits that aren't just physical, including reducing stress and anxiety, improving daily functioning and boosting energy levels. These advantages highlight the importance of people in the UK engaging in regular exercise.

This pie chart highlights that on average, more than 1/3 of the UK population typically engage in less than 1 hour of exercise each week, potentially contributing to health inequalities.

The gap between health and exercise

Some health inequalities exists as some individuals cannot afford resources that support a healthy lifestyle such as gym memberships or fitness classes. A YouGov survey discovered that 11% of adults in the UK has either cancelled or were considering cancelling a gym or other sports or exercise membership due to rising costs.

Rising living costs in the UK have led some individuals to prioritise certain aspects of their lifestyle, with some unable to maintain their previous standard of life. Studies indicate that for those struggling financially, participation in exercise and fitness activities is often one of the first sacrifices made. Financial barriers to fitness resources may limit physical activity among individuals from underprivileged areas.

In some instances, exercise environments or classes are designed to attract specific participants, unintentionally or intentionally excluding others. Writer Leah Sinclair stated that 'the wellness space feels like an exclusive members club that I'm desperately trying to break into'.

This trend could deepen health inequalities, since those who can pay for premium classes are more likely to stay healthy and fit, while those with limited financial resources may exercise less and face greater health risks.

With five out of six reasons for decreased exercise spending linked to money, it highlights that a better financial situation often enables better health, as affordability is a key factor in maintaining an active lifestyle.

Evidence from 2022 suggests that physical inactivity is associated with 1 in 6 deaths in the UK and imposes an estimated annual cost of £7.4 billion in the UK. Compared to the 1960s, people in the UK are now roughly 20% less active, with projections suggesting a 35% decline by 2030 if trends continue. Physical activity is crucial to live a healthy live however, around 1 in 3 men and 1 in 2 women are not active enough for good health.

The impact of deprivation on health inequalities

Individuals in the most deprived areas of the UK are more likely to experience poverty or low incomes, which can limit their ability to pay for exercise and maintain a healthy diet, reinforcing the cycle of health inequalities in the UK.

This graph highlights that individuals in the most deprived areas eat less fruit and vegetables, indicating less healthy diets compared to those in the least deprived areas which negatively impacts living a healthy lifestyle.

Those living in the most deprived areas develop long-term health conditions 10 to 15 years earlier than those in least deprived communities, leading to more years of ill health and have shorter life expectancies. In the 20% most deprived areas, emergency admissions for infectious diseases are nearly double those in the least deprived areas.

This bar chart illustrates that individuals in the most deprived areas are twice as likely to have type 2 diabetes as those in the least deprived areas.

Predicted trends in health inequalities

If current trends continue, health inequalities are likely to persist over the next two decades, with people in the most deprived 10% of areas expected to be diagnosed with major illnesses around 10 years earlier than those in the least deprived areas.

The 10-year gap in healthy life expectancy between the most and least deprived areas is expected to persist through 2040 and individuals in the most deprived areas are projected to be four times more likely to die prematurely by age 70 by 2040.

These projections suggest a growing burden on the NHS, with higher demand for healthcare services leading to increased pressure on resources and the workforce. The health service estimated to be spending as much as £50 billion a year on the effects of deprivation.
