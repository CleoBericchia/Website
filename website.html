The rise in melanoma in the UK: the consequences of ‘extreme tanning’ habits This story highlights that the increasing prevalence of melanoma in the UK can often be considered a result of ‘extreme tanning’ habits of Brits. Both the incidence and mortality rates of melanoma have increased in the UK and understanding the factors contributing to this increase is important.

What is melanoma? Melanoma skin cancer develops when abnormal skin cells multiple in an uncontrolled way. Melanoma may form anywhere on the skin, commonly starting in a mole or in areas like under the nails, on the hands or feet. A common cause of melanoma is too much exposure to ultraviolet light (UV radiation). This can result in sunburn, damaging skin cells and potentially causing them to grow uncontrollably, leading to cancer. Overexposure to sunlight and UV radiation can cause significant skin damage, increasing the risk of melanoma while also leading to wrinkles, fine lines and pigmentation changes, a process also known as photoaging.

Causes of melanoma Some risk factors for developing melanoma cannot be controlled. For example, having a family history of melanoma also doubles the risk of developing melanoma. Also, people that are born with a high number of moles, or moles that are large or unusually shaped are 4-10 times more likely to develop melanoma. A noticeable change in a mole is one of the most common warning signs. Extreme sun exposure can also increase the number of moles on an individual, increasing the likelihood that these moles may become cancerous. High levels of tanning and overexposure to UV radiation have a severely negative impact on the skin, damaging its condition and significantly increasing the risk of melanoma. It is important for individual’s to monitor moles that are dark or uneven in colour and moles that have changed shape or size.

Effects of extreme tanning In the UK, melanoma cases are increasing faster than any other common cancer. Over the past decade, there has been a rise of nearly 50%, with roughly 15,400 people diagnosed each year. A possible explanation to the increase of melanoma in the UK can be explained due to the increased use of extreme tanning methods like sunbeds, nasal sprays and injections. Sunbed use before the age of 35 is linked to a 59% higher risk of melanoma in later years. In the UK, almost 90% of melanoma cases could be avoided by staying safe in the sun and not using sunbeds.

Introduced in 1978, sunbeds have gone through cycles of popularity and decline, however their use continues to grow in the UK, particularly among younger age groups, even with government regulations in place. Melanotan products come in the form of nasal sprays and tanning injections that are unauthorised in the UK due to a lack of testing. They can cause side effects such as nausea, drowsiness, vomiting, and loss of appetite. Tanning injections also carry injection-related risks including HIV/AIDS, nerve damage and hepatitis B. Certain individuals are effectively introducing unregulated substances into their bodies in pursuit of a tan, which not only increases the risk of skin cancer but also involves products that have not undergone sufficient safety testing.

Growth of melanoma in the UK Cancer Research UK predict that melanoma skin cancer incidence rates in the UK are projected to reach around 26,500 cases each year by 2038-2040. Melanoma mortality in the UK is set to rise, with a predicted 44% increase from 2018 figures, according to Melanoma UK, leading to 3,968 deaths by 2040.

The highest incidence rates tend to be seen in older individuals, with around one-third of new case each year in the UK occurring in those aged 75 and above. This pattern is also observed in mortality rates, which rise significantly with age.

Older people face a significantly higher risk of developing melanoma and compared to other cancers, the age-adjusted rate of melanoma is climbing most quickly.

In recent years, there has been a growing number of melanoma cases among adults over 55. This may be partly explained by the fact that sunbeds first emerged during the teenage years of this age group. First appearing in 1978, sunbeds were increasingly advertised by the 1990s which contributed to its growing popularity, but their use decreased in the 2000s amid negative publicity, including labels such as ‘ultraviolet coffins’ and ‘atomic bombs’ which may help explain why the melanoma incidence rates are lower for those under 55. Increased longevity also contributes to extended periods of sun exposure over a lifetime. A further explanation for the growth in melanoma is inadequate knowledge about safe sun exposure. A survey conducted by the British Skin Foundation found that 51% of Brits have accidentally sunburned themselves in 2025.

This survey, highlights a potential issue with sun safety in the UK, showing that many Brits still get sunburnt, which may reflect either careless behaviour or a lack of sun safety education in the UK. Social media and extreme tanning According to research, 60% of young people use sunbeds, with 25% of them doing so regularly. This may be due to the influence of sunbed promotion on social media platforms. The hashtag ‘sunbed’ has been used in more than 500,000 posts across TikTok and Instagram. The use of sunbeds is on the rise in the UK, with contributing factors like the popularity of tanned skin as a beauty standard, the influence of social media influencers and misconceptions that portray sunbeds as safer tanning options. There is a sense of encouragement on social media to use sunbeds, as seen in ‘come for a sunbed with me’ videos shared by influencers.

The use of sunbeds is increasingly marketed as a form of self-care, as some associate it with stress reduction, vitamin D production and improved mental wellbeing. As seen with today’s older population and higher melanoma rates, the same pattern could occur in 30 years as Gen Z age, given their rising use of sunbeds.

In 2021, 18,300 people were diagnosed with melanoma in the UK this figure is projected to rise to 21,300 by 2026.
